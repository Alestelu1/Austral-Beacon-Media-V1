Strait of Magellan

10 documented beacons

The Strait connects the Pacific and Atlantic oceans across 330 nautical miles. Its navigation track holds 30 lighthouses and 12 buoys in total, of which 5 are manned stations with Maritime Traffic Control functions. The original balizamiento project from 1899 considered 7 major lighthouses.

Faro Islotes Evangelistas
1896
● Manned

Western entrance of the Strait. Built on a rocky islet 13 nautical miles from the mainland, it endures winds over 120 km/h and waves exceeding 20 meters. First lit September 18, 1896. Maritime Traffic Control station.

📍 52°24'S · 75°06'W
Faro Bahía San Félix
1907
● Manned

Western sector of the Strait, inaugurated June 1, 1907. One of five manned stations serving as Maritime Traffic Control for vessels transiting the western reaches of the waterway.

📍 52°58'S · 73°57'W
Faro Isla Fairway
1920
● Manned

Inaugurated December 6, 1920. Positioned near the Pacific entrance, it guides vessels into the western section of the Strait and serves as a Maritime Traffic Control post.

📍 52°47'S · 74°45'W
Faro Paso Tortuoso
1900s
● Manned

Located at the narrowest and most complex section of the Strait. Full Maritime Traffic Control station. Vessels over 200m LOA must report their ETA to this station before entering the passage.

📍 53°34'S · 72°27'W
Faro Punta Delgada
1900s
● Manned

Situated at the First Narrows (Primera Angostura), where currents can reach 8 knots at spring tides. Serves as a Maritime Traffic Control station and pilot boarding point for eastbound vessels.

📍 52°27'S · 69°30'W
Faro Punta Dungeness
1899
● Manned

Eastern mouth of the Strait. Inaugurated February 20, 1899. Its light reaches 21 nautical miles (40 km), signaling the Atlantic entrance to the waterway. A 25-meter iron tower, originally destined for Islotes Evangelistas.

📍 52°24'S · 68°26'W
Faro Cabo Espíritu Santo
1968
● Manned

Chilean sovereign lighthouse at the eastern mouth of the Strait, on the north coast of Isla Grande de Tierra del Fuego. Inaugurated August 4, 1968; manned since September 15, 1978. Marks the southern limit of the Strait's Atlantic entrance alongside Punta Dungeness.

📍 52°39'S · 68°36'W
Faro San Isidro
1902
○ Automatic

12-meter concrete tower. Manned until 1950, now fully automated. Equipped with Fresnel lens optics with a range of 15 nautical miles. Today operates as a heritage museum under a concession agreement with Cabo Froward Maritime.

📍 53°34'S · 70°55'W
Faro Cooper Key
—
○ Automatic

Located at the deepest section of the Strait, where the channel reaches 1,080 meters depth. Serves as a key reference point for vessels transiting the central Strait.

📍 53°45'S · 71°58'W
Faro Crosstide
—
○ Automatic

Positioned at the narrowest coastal margin in the Strait — just 5 cable lengths from shore at Paso Tortuoso. Critical navigation reference for large vessels where the track offers the least margin for error.

📍 53°36'S · 72°22'W

Cape Horn

3 documented beacons

The southernmost navigable zone of South America, where the Pacific meets the Atlantic at the Drake Passage. The lighthouses here operate under extreme conditions — persistent gales, waves over 6 meters and rapidly changing weather. All are administered by the Chilean Navy.

Faro Cabo de Hornos
1962
● Manned

Original lighthouse at the southernmost point of Isla Hornos. Inaugurated November 2, 1962. The station includes a meteorological post, radar control room and family housing for the permanent Navy crew assigned to the Alcaldía de Mar.

📍 55°58'S · 67°17'W
Faro Monumental Isla Hornos
1991
● Manned

Monumental lighthouse inaugurated November 17, 1991 at Punta Espolón. Its cast-iron cylindrical tower — with white and red horizontal stripes — was built in 1900 in Viña del Mar and previously served at San Antonio until 1987. Elevation: 57 m above sea level.

📍 55°57'S · 67°13'W
Faro Islas Diego Ramírez
1951
● Manned

"El Faro más Austral del Mundo." Located 100 km southwest of Cape Horn at the threshold of the Drake Passage, on the approach to the Chilean Antarctic Territory. Crews of 3–4 Navy specialists serve 6-month isolated rotations. Wind gusts reach 200 km/h.

📍 56°31'S · 68°43'W

Chilean Antarctica

78 navigation aids

The Chilean Navy has maintained a presence in the Antarctic Territory since its first expedition in 1947. Today the network includes 78 navigation aids — luminous beacons, blind beacons, satellite virtual signals and radiolocation systems — maintained annually during the Antarctic Campaign.

78

Navigation Aids in Chilean Antarctic Territory

Including luminous buoys, blind beacons, virtual satellite signals and radiolocation systems. Maintained by Navy specialists deployed aboard vessels such as the OPV "Marinero Fuentealba" and the icebreaker "Almirante Viel" during annual Antarctic campaigns.

The first Chilean navigation aid in Antarctica was installed in March 1947 at Punta Prat, near Naval Base Arturo Prat — an acetylene-powered metal tower described as "the first Chilean light blinking in Antarctica."

Baliza Punta Prat
1947
◈ Historic Beacon

First Chilean navigation aid installed in Antarctica, March 1947. An acetylene-powered metal tower near what would become Naval Base Arturo Prat, on Greenwich Island. Described by witness Eugenio Orrego Vicuña as "the first Chilean light blinking in Antarctica."

📍 62°28'S · 59°41'W
Base Naval Arturo Prat
1947
● Permanent Station

Chile's permanent Antarctic naval base on Greenwich Island. Operational year-round, it serves as the anchor point for the Gobernación Marítima Antártica and supports maritime signaling throughout the sector.

📍 62°29'S · 59°40'W
Gobernación Marítima — Bahía Fildes
1990s
● Permanent Station

Maritime authority hub on King George Island, established in the 1990s. Staffed year-round by Navy specialists who coordinate traffic with national and international Antarctic stations and maintain navigation aids throughout the sector.

📍 62°12'S · 58°57'W
Base O'Higgins — Señalización
1948
● Permanent Station

Chile's northernmost Antarctic base on the Antarctic Peninsula, operational since February 18, 1948. Navigation aids in the surrounding waters are maintained by Navy specialists during each annual Antarctic Campaign.

📍 63°19'S · 57°53'W
